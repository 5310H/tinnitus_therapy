Tinnitus Research Overview

Evidence behind sound‑based and neuromodulation therapies.

Bimodal Neuromodulation (Lenire‑style)

Bimodal neuromodulation pairs auditory stimulation with electrical tongue stimulation. Large clinical trials and real‑world studies show that this combination can produce clinically meaningful reductions in tinnitus severity, especially in moderate‑to‑severe cases.

Key Findings

  • Real‑world U.S. study (n=212): 91.5% achieved clinically meaningful improvement, with an average 27.8‑point THI reduction.
  • Peer‑reviewed U.S. trial (n=140): 81.8% showed clinically meaningful improvement after 12 weeks.
  • TENT‑A3 clinical trial: Bimodal stimulation outperformed sound‑only therapy, with 70.5% reporting meaningful improvement.
  • Benefits appear strongest in moderate‑to‑severe tinnitus and in patients with high baseline distress.

This site provides only the sound‑only component of auditory stimulation. While sound‑only therapy can help, evidence consistently shows that adding tongue stimulation produces significantly stronger outcomes.

Sources

  • Conlon et al., 2022 — TENT‑A3 Clinical Trial (Scientific Reports)
  • Neuromod Devices — U.S. Real‑World Outcomes Study (2024)
  • Huber et al., 2023 — Bimodal Neuromodulation U.S. Trial

Notch Therapy

Notch Therapy removes a narrow frequency band centered on the tinnitus pitch. The goal is to reduce abnormal neural gain at that frequency and promote cortical reorganization. It is most effective for tonal tinnitus.

Key Findings

  • 2025 Meta‑analysis (14 RCTs, n=793): Notched Music Therapy significantly reduced tinnitus loudness and THI scores compared to conventional music therapy.
  • Randomized controlled trial (2022): Notched Sound Therapy produced significantly greater THI reduction than standard care (β = −10.02).
  • fNIRS neuroimaging study (2022): Notched sound reduced connectivity between auditory cortex and emotional‑processing regions.
  • Hearing‑aid‑based Notch Therapy: Clinical data from Signia devices show reduced tinnitus annoyance over weeks to months.

Notch Therapy is one of the most evidence‑supported sound‑only treatments for tonal tinnitus.

Sources

  • Wang et al., 2025 — Meta‑analysis of Notched Music Therapy
  • Li et al., 2022 — Notched Sound Therapy RCT
  • Chen et al., 2022 — fNIRS Study on Notched Sound
  • Signia — Notch Therapy Clinical Whitepaper

Broadband Sound Therapy

Broadband noise (white, pink, or customized noise) reduces the contrast between tinnitus and the environment, supporting habituation. It is widely used in Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT) and modern sound‑therapy programs.

Key Findings

  • Clinical program (n=200+, 2023): Customized broadband noise matched to hearing loss produced relief in 96% of patients completing a 4‑month program, with an average 23‑point THI reduction.
  • TRT‑style therapy: Hearing‑loss‑equalized broadband noise produced significant improvement in 88% of patients after 4 months.
  • Randomized crossover trial (2022): Broadband noise reduced TFI by ~8.2 points over 8 weeks, outperforming nature sounds.

Broadband noise is one of the most widely validated tinnitus therapies and is effective across many tinnitus subtypes.

Sources

  • Jalilvand et al., 2023 — Customized Broadband Noise Therapy Study
  • Jastreboff & Hazell — TRT Clinical Data
  • Henry et al., 2022 — Broadband Noise vs. Nature Sounds RCT

Decorrelated Noise Therapy

Decorrelated noise uses two channels of noise that are intentionally uncorrelated. The goal is to reduce neural synchrony in auditory pathways, which is believed to contribute to tinnitus perception.

Current Evidence

  • Supported by neural models showing that reducing synchronous firing may reduce tinnitus‑related hyperactivity.
  • Used in several experimental auditory‑stimulation protocols and research tools.
  • Clinical evidence is promising but limited compared to Notch Therapy or broadband noise.
  • Often used as part of multi‑sound therapy programs rather than a standalone validated treatment.

Decorrelated noise remains an experimental but theoretically supported approach aimed at reducing abnormal auditory synchrony.

Sources

  • Schaette & Kempter — Neural Synchrony Models of Tinnitus
  • Eggermont & Roberts — Auditory Cortex Hyperactivity Review
  • Various auditory‑stimulation pilot studies (2018–2024)

Which Therapy Is Right for Me?

Each therapy targets different tinnitus mechanisms. Here’s a quick guide:

  • If your tinnitus is tonal: Notch Therapy or Broadband Noise are strong options.
  • If your tinnitus is moderate–severe or highly distressing: Bimodal Neuromodulation has the strongest evidence.
  • If your tinnitus changes with stress or emotion: Notch Therapy’s cortical‑emotion connectivity effects may help.
  • If you want a general, proven approach: Broadband Noise has the widest clinical support.
  • If you want experimental neural‑targeting: Decorrelated Noise is promising but still emerging.

Most people benefit from combining therapies — for example, broadband noise for daily habituation plus notch therapy for tonal suppression.

Evidence Summary

  • Bimodal Neuromodulation: Strongest clinical evidence; multiple large trials; high responder rates.
  • Notch Therapy: Strong evidence for tonal tinnitus; supported by meta‑analysis and neuroimaging.
  • Broadband Noise: Longstanding clinical support; effective across tinnitus types; core of TRT.
  • Decorrelated Noise: Supported by neural models; limited clinical trials; promising experimental approach.
