Tinnitus Research Overview

Summary of evidence behind Lenire‑style and sound‑based tinnitus therapies.

Lenire (Bimodal Neuromodulation)

Lenire is a tinnitus treatment that combines:

  • Sound stimulation through headphones
  • Mild electrical pulses delivered to the tongue

This dual‑input approach is known as bimodal neuromodulation. Research shows that pairing sound with somatosensory stimulation can promote changes in the auditory system.

Key Findings

  • Clinical trials reported reductions in tinnitus severity for many participants.
  • Sound‑only stimulation also produced improvement, but bimodal stimulation produced stronger effects.
  • Benefits were more pronounced in people with moderate to severe tinnitus.

What This Project Uses

This site provides the sound‑only component of Lenire‑style therapy:

  • Carrier tones
  • Broadband noise
  • Frequency‑shaped noise
  • Amplitude‑modulated noise
  • Tone‑burst patterns

No electrical stimulation is included. This is an experimental sound‑only tool inspired by published research.

Sound Therapy Approaches

Sound therapy is one of the most widely used tinnitus management strategies. It works by reducing contrast between tinnitus and silence, promoting habituation, and altering auditory gain.

Common Sound‑Based Methods

  • White, pink, and brown noise — broadband noise used for masking and habituation.
  • Notch therapy — removes a narrow frequency band around the tinnitus pitch.
  • Decorrelated noise — left/right differences reduce central auditory synchrony.
  • Amplitude‑modulated noise — rhythmic modulation may reduce perceived loudness.
  • Tone‑based therapy — tones matched to tinnitus frequency for targeted stimulation.

Evidence Summary

  • Broadband noise can reduce tinnitus awareness over time.
  • Notch therapy has shown benefit in several studies, especially for tonal tinnitus.
  • Modulated noise and tone‑burst patterns are being explored in newer research.
  • Sound‑only therapy is non‑invasive and widely used in clinical practice.

The tools on this site implement these methods using the Web Audio API, allowing real‑time sound generation in the browser.
